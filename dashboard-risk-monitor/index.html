How To Use The GMI Risk Monitor

Before You Start

This is not a trading model. It is a guide to help you make more informed decisions using a range of tools that you may already use yourself.

At GMI, we do not use this tool for trading assets around swings in liquidity during a cycle. We use it as one of the many tools we use to help us at the beginning or end of a cycle.

This also does not replace the GMI Seasons or asset allocation model which is much more rigorous and nuanced. This model is all about trend and is to help you manage volatility in your portfolio.

No system is perfect. All trend-following approaches will experience whipsaws, false signals, and periods of underperformance. Use this alongside your own judgement.

The Hierarchy of Trend

There is a deliberate hierarchy to the information on this page. Start at the top and work down.

First, what is the Secular Trend? Is the long-term structural direction in your favour? If it is, the odds are with you.

Next, what is Liquidity doing? Global liquidity is the single most important driver of asset prices across the cycle.

Then the Business Cycle: is the real economy confirming or diverging from liquidity? Together these give you the Macro Regime.

Finally, the Weekly Trend is the tactical layer. It helps you manage volatility within the larger move. It is not a trading signal. It tells you when an asset's medium-term momentum has shifted, so you can decide how much volatility you are personally comfortable sitting through.

The Macro Regime Box

This is the most important signal on the page. It combines two inputs: the direction of global liquidity (M2) and the business cycle (ISM Manufacturing).

When both are rising, we are in a Full Cycle Expansion. GMI holds positions with conviction.

When liquidity leads but the economy hasn't confirmed, we are in Early Cycle accumulation.

When liquidity fades but the economy is still strong, it's Late Cycle and caution is warranted.

When both are falling, it's Contraction.

Weekly Trend (Rising / Falling)

Each asset has a weekly trend signal based on the GMI Proprietary Medium-Term Risk Indicator.

Green / Rising means price is above the trend line and the asset is in a confirmed uptrend.

Red / Falling means price has broken below.

These are not trading signals for intraday moves. They help you manage volatility around GMI's strategic positions.

Secular Trend (Rising / Falling)

This is the long-term structural trend. For traditional assets it uses a 60-month (5-year) moving average. For crypto and crypto-adjacent equities it uses log regression channels.

Rising means the asset is in a long-term structural bull market. Falling means the opposite.

An asset can be in a secular uptrend but a weekly downtrend. That simply means it's pulling back within a larger bull move.

Signal Change

When an asset flips from uptrend to downtrend (or vice versa) that week, it will be highlighted with a pulsing New Buy or New Sell badge.

This is the key alert to pay attention to each week. No signal change means the existing trend is intact and no action is needed.

30-Day Volatility & Position Sizing

Each asset in the table now shows its 30-day realised volatility (annualised). This tells you how much the asset has been moving recently.

The colour coding classifies risk: green (under 20%) is Normal Risk, amber (20–40%) is Risky, and orange (above 40%) is High Risk. Crypto and crypto-adjacent equities will almost always sit in the Risky or High Risk bands. That is the nature of the asset class.

Assets with higher long-term volatility tend to deliver higher long-term returns. But they also fall significantly more in down markets. There is no way around this.

If drawdowns in assets like crypto are more than you can stomach, consider volatility-adjusting your position sizes. Smaller positions in higher-volatility assets will reduce your peak-to-trough drawdowns at the cost of some upside.

The goal is to find a level of exposure that lets you stay in the trade through the full cycle rather than panic-selling at the worst possible moment. The best returns come from those who can hold through the volatility, and position sizing is how you make that possible.
